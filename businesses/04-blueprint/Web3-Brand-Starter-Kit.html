Section 03

Logo Direction Worksheet

Complete this before briefing a designer or using an AI design tool. Vague briefs produce vague logos.

Part A — The Foundation

Project name:
Your project name here
How it's pronounced (phonetic):
e.g., "Aye-thee" not "Ath-ee"
Describe your project in one sentence without using "decentralized," "trustless," or "revolutionary":
We let [specific user] do [specific thing] without [specific friction].
3 brands (any industry) whose visual identity you admire — and specifically what you admire about their logo:
1. [Brand] — [What you like about their logo specifically]
2. [Brand] — [What you like]
3. [Brand] — [What you like]

Part B — Logo Type

Which format do you prefer? Check all acceptable options, then select your first choice.
  • Wordmark — Just the name in a specific typeface (e.g., Google, FedEx)
  • Lettermark — Just initials (e.g., IBM, HBO)
  • Symbol mark — Just an icon (e.g., Apple, Twitter)
  • Combination mark — Name + icon together (most common, most flexible)
  • Emblem — Name contained within a badge or shield

Part C — Personality Sliders

For each pair, mark where your brand sits (these inform your designer):
Formal
Casual
Technical
Human
Minimal
Complex
Traditional
Avant-garde
Serious
Playful
Stable
Dynamic
→ Adjust these sliders based on your project. They give your designer a directional brief without prescribing specific visual choices.

Part D — Visual Criteria

THE LOGO SHOULD:
  • Work in a single color (black or white only)
  • Be recognizable at 16×16 pixels (favicon size)
  • Work on both light and dark backgrounds
  • Work on merchandise (t-shirts, stickers)
  • Include a standalone icon that works without the wordmark
  • Feel timeless (could work in 10 years)
THE LOGO SHOULD NOT:
  • Use gradients (hard to reproduce in print/embroidery)
  • Reference existing crypto project logos
  • Use generic imagery (shields, chains, rockets, flames)
  • Require more than 3 colors to render correctly
  • Rely on fine details that disappear at small sizes
  • Hexagons, circuit boards, infinity symbols, abstract swooshes

Part E — Designer Brief Summary

In summary, our logo should:
1. Be [STYLE — simple wordmark / combination mark / etc.]
2. Feel [3 PERSONALITY WORDS from your sliders]
3. Reference [ANYTHING MEANINGFUL — optional]
4. Work at [MINIMUM SIZE] and in [BLACK AND WHITE]
5. NOT look like [YOUR AVOID LIST]
Deliverables to request from your designer: SVG and PNG in at minimum: full color, white, black, on dark background, on light background. Favicon version (simplified for small sizes). Horizontal and vertical lockup if combination mark. Brand guidelines PDF specifying correct usage.

Section 05

Pitch Deck Outline — 12-Slide Structure

For each slide: purpose, what to include, what to avoid, and design guidance.

01
Cover
Make a strong first impression. Establish context immediately.
  • Project name and logo (prominently)
  • One-line description (under 10 words)
  • "Confidential" if appropriate
  • Date and presenter name
Design note: This slide is on screen while you introduce yourself. Clean enough to not compete with you speaking.
02
The Problem
Make the investor feel the problem before you explain your solution.
  • One specific, concrete problem statement (not vague "DeFi is broken")
  • Data that quantifies the problem
  • Who experiences it (your specific user)
  • Why existing solutions fail
100-150 words max on the slide. Data visualization beats paragraph text.
03
The Market
Demonstrate the problem is big enough to warrant the investment.
  • TAM (Total Addressable Market)
  • SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market)
  • SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market)
  • Source for each number
  • Growth trajectory chart
Avoid: Claiming 1% of a trillion-dollar market without explanation.
04
The Solution
Show them what you built.
  • Clear, jargon-free description of what your product does
  • Screenshot, demo, or mockup
  • Mechanism: how does it work in plain language
  • Why your approach beats alternatives
75-100 words. Let the visual carry weight. This is not a product spec.
05
Product (What Exists Today)
Separate vision from current reality. Investors need to know what's live.
  • What is live right now (specific)
  • What is in development (realistic timelines)
  • Screenshots of live product
  • One key user flow explained simply
Never mix live features with roadmap items without clear distinction.
06
Traction
The most important slide. Show proof. Priority order: DAU/WAU/MAU, TVL, Revenue, Transactions, Community size (last resort — easy to fake).
  • Your single strongest metric large and prominent
  • Supporting metrics (3-5 max)
  • MoM or WoW growth charts
  • Logos of notable partners/exchanges
Avoid vanity metrics without context. Never compare to peak metrics without current state.
07
Business Model
Explain how the project sustains and grows economically.
  • How the protocol generates revenue
  • The token's role in the economic model
  • Treasury design and runway
  • How value flows between participants
Investors can spot unsustainable models. Address sustainability proactively.
08
Token
Explain the token clearly and honestly.
  • Token name and ticker
  • Total supply and current circulating supply
  • Token utility (what it actually does)
  • Distribution visualization
  • Vesting schedule highlights
Answer clearly: "Why does this need a token?" Specific and functional, not "decentralization."
09
Team
Build conviction that these are the right people.
  • Core team with roles
  • For each: one most-relevant credential
  • Specific experience relevant to this project
  • Advisors (only if genuinely advising)
Format: Photo (if comfortable), Name, Role, 1-line credential, 1-line "why they're here."
10
Competitive Landscape
Show you understand the competition honestly.
  • Primary competitors (3-5)
  • Comparison on dimensions that matter to users
  • Your honest strengths and weaknesses
  • Why the market supports another player
Avoid charts where you're green on everything. Investors don't believe these.
11
Roadmap
Show where the project is going and that you have a plan.
  • Q-by-Q milestones for next 12-18 months
  • Clear committed vs. aspirational distinction
  • Major milestones tied to use of funds
  • What "success" looks like by end of period
Visual timeline beats bullet list. Avoid vague milestones like "platform improvements."
12
The Ask
Make a specific, clear request.
  • How much you're raising
  • Round structure (SAFE / SAFT / token warrant)
  • Use of proceeds (pie chart or 3-4 categories)
  • What this funding buys (time + milestones)
  • How to engage: contact info
The golden rule: if you leave unclear about how much you want and what for, you've lost the meeting.

Copy Framework — The 4 Lines That Matter

The Opening Line
Bad: "[PROJECT] is a decentralized platform for [vague thing]."
Good: "[PROJECT] lets [SPECIFIC USER] do [SPECIFIC THING] without [SPECIFIC FRICTION]."
The Traction Line
Bad: "Growing community of engaged members."
Good: "[SPECIFIC NUMBER] [users/wallets/transactions] in [TIMEFRAME], up [X]% month-over-month."
The Token Line
Bad: "[$TICKER] is the native governance token of the protocol."
Good: "[$TICKER] holders [vote on fee distribution / earn X% of protocol revenue / access private feature]."
The Team Line
Bad: "Our team brings decades of combined experience in blockchain and technology."
Good: "[FOUNDER 1] previously [SPECIFIC CREDENTIAL]. [FOUNDER 2] built [SPECIFIC THING]."
Section 07

Brand Voice Worksheet

Your brand voice is how your project sounds in every piece of text. Without a defined voice, you sound different across team members and seasons — which destroys trust.

The 4-Attribute Model

Define your brand voice using 4 attributes. For each: name it, describe it, give an ON-voice example, give an OFF-voice example.

Direct
We say what we mean without hedging or qualifying every statement. Means: directness. Doesn't mean: rude or dismissive.
// on voice
"Our audit found 2 minor issues. Both are fixed. Full report linked."
// off voice
"We wanted to take a moment to share some important information regarding our recent security review process."
Specific
We use real numbers and concrete examples. Means: precision. Doesn't mean: exhaustive detail for its own sake.
// on voice
"47,000 wallets. $12M TVL. 3-month-old protocol."
// off voice
"We've seen significant growth and strong community adoption since our successful launch."
Confident
We own our point of view. Means: confidence. Doesn't mean: arrogant or dismissive of alternatives.
// on voice
"We chose Solana. Fast finality, low fees, growing developer ecosystem. Easy call."
// off voice
"We carefully considered multiple blockchain options and ultimately selected a chain that we believe best serves our needs."
Honest
We acknowledge problems and limitations. Means: transparency. Doesn't mean: self-flagellation or over-sharing.
// on voice
"It was a rough week. Bridge bug found, 4 hours to fix, $0 lost. Here's what we changed."
// off voice
"We deeply apologize for any inconvenience this may have caused our valued community members."
